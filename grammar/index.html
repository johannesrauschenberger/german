1. Nouns

1.1 Capitalisation

Unlike English and most other languages, German capitalizes all nouns, not just names of people and countries, languages etc.

1.2 Gender

German nouns have one of three genders in the singular, masculine (der), feminine (die) , neuter (das) and one in the plural (die).
der Mann, die Frau, das Kind / die Menschen
There aren’t many rules as to what gender a noun could have, so they mostly need to simply be memorized. As a general (but not 100% reliable) rule of thumb, if a noun ends in -e, it is most likely feminine. There are, however, plenty of exceptions to this. More reliable is the following:
-er endings for people or things that come from verbs are usually masculine (-dor ending in PT/ES), der Computer, der Drucker, der Lehrer
-ung/-heit and -keit endings for German nouns usually mean it’s feminine
-tion and -tät endings for foreign loan words also mean the word is feminine
-chen and -lein (diminutive) are always neuter, das
